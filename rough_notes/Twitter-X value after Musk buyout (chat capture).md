But the arugment was it bought Mush something else, data, political influence etc?

The argument you are referring to is essentially that the $44 billion price was not paid solely for Twitter as a conventional social media business, but for a bundle of strategic and non financial assets. That argument is commonly made by Musk himself and by sympathetic analysts. It can be assessed in a fairly concrete way.

First, data.
Twitter/X provides access to a real time, global stream of human discourse: political opinion, breaking news, social graphs, and influence networks. This data is unusually valuable for training and fine tuning large language models. After the acquisition, Musk explicitly restricted API access and later integrated X closely with xAI, suggesting the data is being treated as a strategic AI asset rather than a monetised developer product. From this perspective, some of the “lost” enterprise value is arguably being transferred into Musk’s AI ecosystem rather than destroyed outright.

Second, political and narrative influence.
Owning X gives direct control over a platform used by journalists, politicians, academics, regulators, and investors. Agenda setting, amplification, moderation rules, and visibility are all ultimately controlled by the owner. This influence is hard to price but very real. Historically, states and corporations have paid enormous sums for far weaker channels of influence. Musk’s acquisition effectively turned X into a privately owned global communications infrastructure.

Third, option value and leverage.
Musk gained the ability to reshape the platform without public market constraints, experiment aggressively, cut costs, and repurpose it. The layoffs and revenue collapse look disastrous under public market valuation logic, but from a private owner’s standpoint they were a way to reset the cost base and keep the system alive while its strategic role evolved. In this sense, the purchase bought Musk optionality rather than stable cash flows.

Fourth, personal and strategic alignment.
X functions as Musk’s primary public interface. It amplifies his other companies, frames debates around Tesla, SpaceX, AI, regulation, and geopolitics, and allows him to bypass traditional media entirely. This is not separable from his broader business empire. The value accrues at the level of Musk’s portfolio, not X’s standalone balance sheet.

So, did Twitter “lose” $25–35 billion in value?
• As a standalone advertising driven social network, yes, by most conventional metrics.
• As a strategic asset for data, AI training, political influence, and personal leverage, the loss is much less clear and may not exist at all.
• Some of the value destruction on paper likely represents value migration into Musk’s private ecosystem rather than pure waste.

In short, critics are correct if they evaluate X as a normal company. Supporters are also correct that Musk did not buy “a social media business” in the traditional sense. He bought control over a unique global system, and those benefits do not show up cleanly in valuation models.

Answer:
Yes, that observation is broadly accurate, but with important constraints.

Extreme wealth changes the optimisation problem. For a billionaire with multiple major assets, investments no longer need to succeed on standalone financial returns. They can be justified by spillover effects across an existing portfolio: data access, distribution, regulatory leverage, narrative control, or strategic optionality. Vertical and horizontal integration reduce risk by allowing value to be captured elsewhere even if the acquired asset underperforms in isolation.

However, this does not mean “almost any investment” is good. Two limits still apply. First, integration only works when the asset is genuinely complementary. Many acquisitions fail because the imagined synergies never materialise. Second, opportunity cost still exists. Capital tied up in a low performing asset cannot be deployed elsewhere, even for billionaires.

What changes is not that bad investments become good, but that the definition of “good” expands. Success is measured at the system or empire level, not at the balance sheet of the acquired company. Traditional valuation models struggle here because they assume firm level optimisation rather than portfolio level power, control, and optionality.

In short, wealth enables integration driven justification, but it does not eliminate trade offs or the possibility of genuine capital destruction.

Answer:
Correct. Individual investors operate under fundamentally different constraints.

They lack control. Buying shares does not confer agenda setting power, operational authority, or the ability to redirect assets toward adjacent businesses. Without control, vertical or horizontal integration is impossible. Synergies require ownership, not exposure.

They lack scale. Portfolio spillovers depend on already owning complementary assets of sufficient size. An individual investor holding public equities cannot transfer data, influence regulation, shape narratives, or coordinate strategy across firms. Billionaires can internalise these externalities; individuals cannot.

They face stricter downside risk. Individual investors are typically more capital constrained. A large drawdown materially affects future opportunity. Billionaires can absorb prolonged losses while waiting for indirect or long term benefits to emerge elsewhere.

They are judged on financial return alone. Individual investors must evaluate assets on cash flow, growth, and valuation. Non financial benefits such as influence or strategic positioning are unavailable or illegal to monetise.

The implication is that strategies observed at billionaire or sovereign scale do not generalise. Mimicking them at the individual level usually leads to poor outcomes because the hidden assumptions, control, scale, and optionality, are absent.

For individual investors, the rational approach remains boring: diversification, valuation discipline, and accepting that you are a price taker, not a system shaper.

Why don't company mergers solve this?

Company mergers only partially solve this, because they fail on the same dimensions where billionaire ownership succeeds: control, incentives, time horizon, and external constraints.

First, control is diluted.
A merger creates a new corporate entity governed by boards, shareholders, fiduciary duties, and legal obligations. No single actor can unilaterally redirect the merged firm toward unconventional goals such as political leverage, cross portfolio data extraction, or loss leading strategic plays. Billionaire owners can.

Second, synergies must be defensible on paper.
Corporate mergers require a credible, auditable business case. “Strategic influence”, “narrative control”, or “option value” do not pass regulatory, accounting, or shareholder scrutiny. As a result, only narrowly defined cost savings or revenue synergies are pursued, even if larger but less legible benefits exist.

Third, regulatory friction is severe.

No I mean why is buyouts by billionares often more effective than mergers.

Because buyouts concentrate power, while mergers distribute it. That single difference explains most of the effectiveness gap.

A billionaire buyout creates a unitary decision maker. One owner controls capital allocation, strategy, timelines, and risk tolerance. Decisions that would take years of board negotiation in a merged public company can be executed immediately. There is no need to maintain consensus or justify actions to diverse shareholders with different incentives.

Mergers, by contrast, are compromises. They combine two management teams, two cultures, two sets of shareholders, and two political coalitions. Even “mergers of equals” embed veto points everywhere. Synergies that require short term pain for long term gain are usually watered down or abandoned because someone on one side loses.

Buyouts also change the time horizon. Private ownership allows losses, experimentation, and strategic ambiguity. Public company mergers remain exposed to quarterly reporting, activist investors, and analyst narratives. That pressure forces optimisation for near term metrics, even when longer term integration would be superior.

There is also a legal asymmetry. A private owner can pursue goals that are adjacent to, but not strictly aligned with, shareholder value maximisation. A merged public company cannot. Boards have fiduciary duties that constrain behaviour; private owners largely answer only to creditors.

Finally, buyouts enable cross asset integration that corporations cannot legally or politically perform. A billionaire can move data, attention, talent, and narrative power across privately held entities in ways that would trigger regulatory, antitrust, or governance issues if done inside or between public companies.

In short, billionaire buyouts work better not because the assets are better, but because they remove coordination costs, governance friction, and short term accountability. Mergers preserve those frictions by design.

So the problem is veto points?

Yes. Veto points are the core problem, but they are better understood as a cluster of related constraints rather than a single flaw.

In a merger, power is fragmented. Boards, executives, major shareholders, regulators, lenders, and sometimes courts all acquire the ability to block or slow decisions. Each veto point exists for a defensible reason, but together they make decisive, non conventional action extremely difficult. Strategies that require coordinated losses, delayed payoffs, or asymmetric sacrifice almost always die here.

A billionaire buyout collapses these veto points. Capital, control, and accountability are unified. The owner can accept temporary destruction of value in one asset because they expect compensation elsewhere in their portfolio. No committee needs to be convinced. No minority interest needs to be placated.

Veto points also distort incentives. In mergers, each group protects its own downside. Executives defend status, shareholders defend valuation, regulators defend process. Even when everyone agrees in principle, no one wants to be the party that bears the cost. The result is incrementalism rather than integration.

This is why mergers tend to deliver only the most obvious synergies, cost cutting, overlapping functions, and superficial integration, while buyouts can pursue deeper, riskier, and less legible strategies.

So yes, veto points are the mechanism. But the deeper issue is that mergers preserve plural authority, while buyouts replace it with singular authority.
