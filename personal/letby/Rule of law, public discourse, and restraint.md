**Rule of law, public discourse, and restraint**

- The courts determine guilt and sentence. That is their role, not ours.
    
- Respecting the judicial process means recognising the legal status of a conviction and using proper routes (appeals, review bodies) to challenge it.
    
- But respect for the courts does **not** require silence or blind acceptance.

- From outside the process, individual cases can become absorbing because they look like serious truth-seeking while remaining structurally unresolvable.

    - no full disclosure

    - no cross-examination

    - no authority to settle uncertainty

    - no real connection between your conclusion and the outcome

- That is why this kind of engagement can feel important while also feeling empty: it borrows the form of legal reasoning without the role, tools, or endpoint that make it coherent.

- That does not make all public engagement illegitimate. Narrower forms still matter:
      
    - Reasoned, evidence-based criticism is legitimate.
         
    - Investigative journalism plays an important role in identifying potential errors.
         
    - Even within the judiciary, disagreement is normal and structured.
        
- What should be rejected is **extra-judicial behaviour on either side**:

    - Public pressure can surface issues, uncover evidence, or prompt scrutiny, but it should not itself be a reason for appeal or release.

    - If attention starts driving outcomes, cases are decided by noise, narrative force, and media salience rather than legal merit.
     
    - slogan campaigns that attempt to acquit in the court of public opinion
         
    - dehumanising or punitive rhetoric that goes beyond the sentence imposed
        
- The key distinction is not “support vs doubt”, but:

    - Some imperfection is part of the system's design: finality, consistency, and procedural fairness all require hard stopping points.

    - From the outside, the meaningful contributions are limited: either surface genuinely new evidence or think at the level of institutional reform. Otherwise, restraint is the right response.
     
    - serious, disciplined scrutiny
         
    - vs shallow, emotive, or performative commentary
        

**Conclusion:**  
Respecting the rule of law means not substituting your own verdict, but it does not mean abandoning criticism. It requires restraint, consistency, and a commitment to evidence over noise.
