## Thirlwall Inquiry – Core Analytical Gap

### Summary

There appears to be a central unresolved tension in the Inquiry’s approach:

- The criminal case relies heavily on **clinical interpretation of contemporaneous medical records**.
    
- Those same records were **available to clinicians at the time**.
    
- Yet the Inquiry does not clearly explain **why those clinicians did not escalate concerns earlier**, despite now suggesting that concerns _should_ have been raised sooner.
    

This creates a potential explanatory gap at the heart of the Inquiry’s own terms of reference.

---

### The Core Problem

The Inquiry asks:

> When should concerns have been raised, and were opportunities missed?

But answering this requires two steps:

1. **Identify the relevant clinical signs available at the time**
    
2. **Explain why those signs did not trigger escalation by experienced clinicians**
    

At present, the Inquiry appears to:

- imply that **concerning features existed**
    
- suggest that **earlier escalation was warranted**
    

without clearly specifying:

- what those features were in real-time terms
    
- why they were sufficient to justify escalation
    
- why they were not recognised or acted upon
    

---

### Logical Tension

There are only a few coherent positions:

**A. Signals were not clearly actionable at the time**

- Each case had plausible explanations
    
- Pattern only visible in hindsight
    
- → Delay in escalation is understandable
    

**B. Signals were actionable and should have triggered escalation**

- Repeated unexplained collapses
    
- Inconsistent clinical course
    
- → Requires explanation of why clinicians did not act
    

**C. Signals became actionable only cumulatively**

- Threshold crossed at some point
    
- → Inquiry must identify _when_ and _why_
    

The Inquiry risks drifting between these positions without clearly committing to one.

---

### Key Distinction

The Inquiry appears to be trying to avoid:

> Reconstructing or re-testing the clinical case underlying the convictions

Instead, it focuses on:

- **flow of concern**
    
- **decision-making processes**
    
- **system response**
    

However, this separation is unstable:

- Claiming that concerns should have been raised earlier  
    implicitly depends on
    
- the idea that **recognisable concerning features existed at the time**
    

---

### Risk of Hindsight Bias

If the Inquiry relies (implicitly) on later clinical interpretations, it risks:

- treating patterns as “obvious” that were only visible after aggregation
    
- holding clinicians to a **retrospective standard of knowledge**
    

This would be analytically weak and potentially unfair.

---

### Alternative Interpretations

If earlier escalation was warranted, explanations may include:

- **Ambiguity of signals** (no clear trigger)
    
- **System failure** (communication, hierarchy, culture)
    
- **Cognitive factors** (normalisation, anchoring, uncertainty tolerance)
    

But the Inquiry must **explicitly identify and justify** which applies.

---

### Key Unanswered Question

> If the relevant clinical signs were sufficiently clear to justify earlier escalation, why did experienced clinicians not recognise or act on them at the time?

Without answering this, the Inquiry risks:

- implying failure without analysing its basis
    
- leaving a gap between conclusion and reasoning
    

---

### Clean Formulation

> If the Inquiry concludes that concerns should have been raised earlier, it must specify the contemporaneous clinical features that justified escalation and explain why those features did not lead to earlier action. Without this, it does not fully answer its own question.
